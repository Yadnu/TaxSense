# IRS Publication 17: Your Federal Income Tax — Income Types, Filing Requirements & Standard Deductions

*Source: IRS Publication 17 (Tax Year 2024). For authoritative guidance, visit irs.gov.*

---

## Who Must File a Federal Tax Return

Generally, you must file a federal income tax return if your gross income is at or above certain thresholds based on your filing status and age.

**2024 Filing Thresholds (Tax Year 2024, returns due April 15, 2025):**

- Single under 65: $14,600
- Single 65 or older: $16,550
- Married Filing Jointly, both under 65: $29,200
- Married Filing Jointly, one spouse 65 or older: $30,750
- Married Filing Jointly, both 65 or older: $32,300
- Married Filing Separately (any age): $5
- Head of Household under 65: $21,900
- Head of Household 65 or older: $23,850
- Qualifying Surviving Spouse under 65: $29,200
- Qualifying Surviving Spouse 65 or older: $30,750

Even if your income is below these thresholds, you may want to file to claim a refund of withheld taxes or claim refundable credits such as the Earned Income Credit or Child Tax Credit.

---

## Filing Status Definitions

Your filing status determines your standard deduction, tax rate, and eligibility for certain credits and deductions.

**Single:** You are unmarried, legally separated, or divorced under a final decree as of the last day of the tax year.

**Married Filing Jointly (MFJ):** You are married and both you and your spouse agree to file a joint return. On a joint return, you and your spouse report your combined income and deduct combined expenses.

**Married Filing Separately (MFS):** Married taxpayers who choose to file separate returns. This status generally results in higher taxes than MFJ and limits certain deductions and credits. You cannot claim the Earned Income Credit, education credits, or the student loan interest deduction if you use MFS.

**Head of Household (HoH):** You are unmarried (or considered unmarried) and paid more than half the cost of keeping up a home for a qualifying person. HoH status provides a higher standard deduction than Single.

**Qualifying Surviving Spouse (formerly Qualifying Widow(er)):** For the two years following the year your spouse died, if you have a dependent child, you may be able to use the tax rates and standard deduction that apply to MFJ filers.

## Standard Deduction Amounts for Tax Year 2024

The standard deduction reduces your taxable income. Most taxpayers take the standard deduction rather than itemizing.

| Filing Status | Standard Deduction (2024) |
|---|---|
| Single | $14,600 |
| Married Filing Jointly | $29,200 |
| Married Filing Separately | $14,600 |
| Head of Household | $21,900 |
| Qualifying Surviving Spouse | $29,200 |

**Additional Standard Deduction (Age 65+ or Blind):**
- Single or Head of Household: +$1,950 per qualifying condition
- All other filing statuses: +$1,550 per qualifying condition (per spouse for MFJ)

## Types of Income Subject to Federal Tax

### Wages, Salaries, and Tips

All compensation you receive for services performed is taxable income. This includes:
- Wages and salaries reported on Form W-2
- Tips (including cash tips not reported to your employer)
- Bonuses, commissions, and severance pay
- Sick pay (if paid by your employer)
- Vacation pay
- Fringe benefits (unless specifically excluded)

### Interest Income

Interest is generally taxable in the year you receive it or constructively receive it. Report interest from:
- Bank savings accounts, CDs, and money market accounts (Form 1099-INT)
- US Treasury bills, notes, and bonds (taxable federally, exempt from state tax)
- Corporate bonds
- Savings bonds (you may defer interest until redemption for Series EE/I bonds)

**Tax-exempt interest:** Interest from most state and municipal bonds is exempt from federal income tax but may be subject to the Alternative Minimum Tax (AMT). It is still reported on your return.

### Dividend Income

Dividends are distributions of a corporation's earnings. There are two types:

**Ordinary Dividends:** Taxed at ordinary income rates. Reported in Box 1a of Form 1099-DIV.

**Qualified Dividends:** Taxed at preferential long-term capital gains rates (0%, 15%, or 20%). Reported in Box 1b of Form 1099-DIV. To be qualified, dividends must be paid by a US corporation or a qualified foreign corporation, and you must have held the stock for more than 60 days during the 121-day period beginning 60 days before the ex-dividend date.

### Capital Gains and Losses

A capital gain or loss occurs when you sell a capital asset (stocks, bonds, real estate, etc.) for more or less than your adjusted basis (typically your cost).

**Short-term capital gains:** Assets held one year or less. Taxed at ordinary income rates.

**Long-term capital gains:** Assets held more than one year. Subject to preferential rates:
- 0% if your taxable income is in the 10% or 12% bracket
- 15% for most taxpayers
- 20% for high-income taxpayers (taxable income above $518,900 for single filers in 2024)

**Capital loss deduction:** You can deduct capital losses against capital gains. If you have a net capital loss, you can deduct up to $3,000 per year against ordinary income. Remaining losses carry forward to future years.

### Business and Self-Employment Income

If you are self-employed or carry on a trade or business as a sole proprietor, your business income is reported on Schedule C. Net profit from self-employment is subject to both regular income tax AND self-employment tax (15.3% on the first $168,600 of net earnings; 2.9% above that for tax year 2024).

You can deduct ordinary and necessary business expenses. Common deductions include:
- Home office deduction (simplified method: $5 per square foot, up to 300 sq ft)
- Business use of vehicle (standard mileage rate: 67 cents per mile in 2024)
- Business insurance, supplies, advertising
- Section 179 expensing and bonus depreciation for business equipment

### Rental Income and Expenses

Rental income from real property is taxable. You must report all rental income, including advance rent and payments for canceling a lease. You can deduct rental expenses such as:
- Mortgage interest and property taxes
- Depreciation (residential: 27.5-year straight-line)
- Repairs and maintenance (but not improvements, which are capitalized)
- Insurance, utilities paid by landlord, property management fees

**Passive Activity Rules:** Rental activities are generally considered passive. Losses can only offset passive income. However, if you actively participate in rental management, you may deduct up to $25,000 of rental losses against non-passive income (subject to income phase-out starting at $100,000 MAGI).

### Social Security Benefits

Whether your Social Security benefits are taxable depends on your "combined income":
Combined Income = Adjusted Gross Income + Nontaxable Interest + 50% of Social Security Benefits

- If combined income is below $25,000 (single) or $32,000 (MFJ): benefits are not taxable.
- If between $25,000–$34,000 (single) or $32,000–$44,000 (MFJ): up to 50% may be taxable.
- If above $34,000 (single) or $44,000 (MFJ): up to 85% may be taxable.

### Unemployment Compensation

Unemployment compensation is fully taxable federal income. You should receive Form 1099-G showing the amount you received. You can choose to have federal income tax withheld from your unemployment payments by submitting Form W-4V.

### Alimony

**Post-2018 divorce agreements:** Alimony received under divorce or separation agreements executed after December 31, 2018, is NOT included in gross income (and is not deductible by the payer).

**Pre-2019 divorce agreements:** Alimony received is taxable income to the recipient and deductible by the payer (for agreements executed before January 1, 2019, that have not been modified to adopt the new rules).

### Retirement Income

**Traditional IRA and 401(k) distributions:** Generally fully taxable because contributions were pre-tax. Early withdrawals (before age 59½) are subject to an additional 10% penalty tax (with exceptions).

**Roth IRA distributions:** Qualified distributions from a Roth IRA are entirely tax-free (contributions were after-tax). Qualified means the account is at least 5 years old and you are 59½ or older, disabled, or a first-time homebuyer (up to $10,000 lifetime).

**Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs):** Starting at age 73, you must take annual minimum distributions from traditional IRAs and most employer retirement plans. The amount is calculated based on your account balance and life expectancy tables published by the IRS.

### Gambling Winnings

All gambling winnings are taxable and must be reported on Form 1040, including lottery winnings, casino winnings, and winnings from horse races and poker. You may deduct gambling losses, but only to the extent of gambling winnings, and only if you itemize deductions.

## Key Adjustments to Income (Above-the-Line Deductions)

These deductions reduce your Adjusted Gross Income (AGI) and can be claimed regardless of whether you itemize.

**Student Loan Interest Deduction:** Up to $2,500 of student loan interest paid per year. Phases out at MAGI of $75,000–$90,000 (single) or $155,000–$185,000 (MFJ) in 2024.

**Health Savings Account (HSA) Deduction:** Contributions to an HSA are deductible if made outside payroll. 2024 contribution limits: $4,150 (self-only coverage) or $8,300 (family coverage), plus $1,000 catch-up for age 55+.

**Self-Employed Health Insurance Deduction:** Self-employed individuals can deduct 100% of health insurance premiums paid for themselves and their families (limited to net self-employment income).

**Alimony Paid (pre-2019 agreements):** Deductible for divorce agreements executed before January 1, 2019.

**IRA Contributions:** Contributions to a traditional IRA may be deductible depending on your income and whether you are covered by a workplace retirement plan. 2024 IRA contribution limit: $7,000 ($8,000 if age 50 or older).

## Common Refundable and Non-Refundable Tax Credits

### Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC)
A refundable credit for low-to-moderate income workers, especially those with children. The maximum credit for tax year 2024:
- No children: $632
- 1 child: $4,213
- 2 children: $6,960
- 3 or more children: $7,830

Investment income must be $11,600 or less to qualify.

### Child Tax Credit (CTC)
Up to $2,000 per qualifying child under age 17. Up to $1,700 is refundable (Additional Child Tax Credit) for tax year 2024. Phases out at $200,000 MAGI (single) and $400,000 (MFJ).

### Child and Dependent Care Credit
A non-refundable credit for expenses paid to care for a qualifying child or dependent while you work or look for work. Up to 35% of $3,000 (one qualifying person) or $6,000 (two or more) in expenses. The percentage decreases as income increases.

### American Opportunity Tax Credit (AOTC)
Up to $2,500 per eligible student per year for the first four years of post-secondary education. 40% is refundable (up to $1,000). Phases out at $80,000–$90,000 MAGI (single) or $160,000–$180,000 (MFJ).

### Lifetime Learning Credit
20% of the first $10,000 in qualified education expenses (max $2,000 per return). Non-refundable. No limit on number of years. Same income limits as AOTC.
