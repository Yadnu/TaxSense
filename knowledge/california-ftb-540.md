# California State Income Tax — FTB Form 540 Instructions and Residency Rules

*Source: California FTB Publication 1005, Form 540 Instructions, and FTB Publication 1031 (Tax Year 2024). For authoritative guidance, visit ftb.ca.gov.*

---

## Overview of California Income Tax

California has one of the highest individual income tax rates in the United States. California residents are taxed on all income from all sources, regardless of where earned. Nonresidents are taxed only on California-source income. Part-year residents are taxed on income received while a California resident plus California-source income received while a nonresident.

**California Tax Return Due Date:** April 15, 2025 (for tax year 2024). California automatically grants a 6-month extension to file (to October 15, 2025) if you owe no tax or pay the balance due by April 15. Unlike the federal extension, California does not require filing Form FTB 3519 unless you owe tax.

---

## California Residency Definitions

### Resident
A California resident is an individual who is:
1. **Domiciled in California** — California is your permanent home to which you intend to return, even if you are temporarily outside California; OR
2. **Present in California for other than a temporary or transitory purpose** — A safe harbor: if you spend more than 9 months of the year in California, you are presumed to be a resident.

**Domicile vs. Residence:** You can have only one domicile at a time, but you may be a resident of multiple states simultaneously. California aggressively asserts residency for tax purposes.

### Nonresident
An individual who is not a California resident. Nonresidents are subject to California tax only on income derived from California sources, including:
- Wages and salaries for services performed in California
- Income from a business, trade, or profession carried on in California
- Income from the sale of California real property
- California-source rental income
- Gains from the sale of a partnership interest if the partnership did business in California

### Part-Year Resident
An individual who is a California resident for part of the tax year and a nonresident for the rest. This commonly applies to people who moved into or out of California during the year. Part-year residents file a single Form 540NR (Nonresident or Part-Year Resident Return).

## California Standard Deduction (Form 540)

California's standard deduction is significantly lower than the federal standard deduction:

| Filing Status | California Standard Deduction (2024) |
|---|---|
| Single | $5,202 |
| Married Filing Jointly | $10,404 |
| Married Filing Separately | $5,202 |
| Head of Household | $10,404 |
| Qualifying Surviving Spouse | $10,404 |

Because California's standard deduction is so low, many taxpayers who take the federal standard deduction will find it worthwhile to itemize on their California return.

## Key Differences Between California and Federal Tax

### Items Taxed Differently in California

**Social Security Benefits:** California does NOT tax Social Security benefits. This is a significant difference from federal treatment (where up to 85% may be taxable).

**California SDI (State Disability Insurance):** SDI deducted from your paycheck is a California-specific tax. It is NOT deductible on your California return. However, if you itemize for federal purposes, you can deduct SDI as a state tax on Schedule A (subject to the $10,000 SALT cap).

**IRA/Pension Income:** California generally follows federal treatment — traditional IRA distributions are taxable; Roth IRA qualified distributions are not.

**Unemployment Insurance (UI):** California DOES NOT tax unemployment compensation, unlike the federal government which taxes it fully.

**Moving Expenses:** California allows a deduction for moving expenses for military members in some circumstances. For non-military, California does NOT allow a deduction (same as federal after TCJA).

**Mortgage Interest Deduction:** California follows the federal limit of $750,000 in acquisition debt ($1 million for loans taken before December 16, 2017).

**SALT Deduction Cap:** California residents are subject to the federal $10,000 cap on state and local tax (SALT) deductions for federal purposes. California itself has no cap on deducting taxes paid to other states.

**Capital Gains:** California taxes all capital gains as ordinary income — there are NO preferential long-term capital gains rates in California. All gains are taxed at your regular California marginal rate.

**Pass-Through Entity (PTE) Tax:** California allows a PTE tax election that lets pass-through entities (partnerships, S-corps, LLCs) pay state income tax at the entity level. Owners receive a credit on their California return. This is a workaround for the federal SALT cap.

**529 Plan Contributions:** California does NOT allow a state deduction for contributions to 529 college savings plans (unlike many other states). However, earnings grow tax-free for qualified educational expenses.

## California Itemized Deductions (Schedule CA)

When you itemize deductions for California, note these key differences from federal:

**Medical Expenses:** California allows a deduction for medical expenses exceeding 7.5% of California AGI (same threshold as federal).

**State and Local Taxes:** California allows you to deduct state income taxes paid to another state on your California return (as a credit on Schedule S). On your California return, you deduct federal income taxes paid — but WAIT: California does NOT allow a deduction for federal income taxes. However, California does allow you to deduct state taxes paid to other states, subject to certain limitations.

**Charitable Contributions:** California follows federal rules for charitable deductions.

**Gambling Losses:** California does NOT allow a deduction for gambling losses. If you had gambling winnings, you must report them as California income but cannot offset them with losses.

**Miscellaneous Deductions:** California allows miscellaneous itemized deductions subject to the 2% of AGI floor (California did NOT adopt the federal TCJA elimination of these deductions). This includes unreimbursed employee business expenses, tax preparation fees, and investment expenses.

## California Exemption Credits (Form 540, Lines 32–35)

California uses nonrefundable credits (called exemption credits) instead of personal exemptions:

| Exemption Type | Credit Amount (2024) |
|---|---|
| Single / MFS / Dependent | $144 |
| Married Filing Jointly | $288 |
| Head of Household | $144 |
| Blind | $144 additional |
| Senior (65+) | $144 additional |
| Each dependent | $433 |

These credits are phased out for high-income taxpayers (starting at $100,000 California AGI for single filers and $200,000 for joint filers, reduced by $6 per $2,500 of income over the threshold).

## California-Specific Credits and Programs

### Young Child Tax Credit (YCTC)
A refundable credit of up to $1,117 per qualifying child under age 6. Available to taxpayers with earned income and California AGI below approximately $30,000 (adjusted annually).

### Child and Dependent Care Expenses Credit
California provides its own credit (in addition to the federal credit) of up to 50% of eligible expenses for taxpayers with income under $40,000, decreasing to 43% for income $40,001–$70,000, and so on down to a minimum of 8% for income above $100,000.

### Earned Income Tax Credit (CalEITC)
California has its own refundable Earned Income Tax Credit that supplements the federal EITC. For tax year 2024, the maximum CalEITC is:
- No children: $285
- 1 child: $1,900
- 2 children: $3,137
- 3 or more children: $3,529

The California EITC has lower income limits than the federal EITC and does not allow self-employment income to exceed $30,931 for eligibility.

### Renter's Credit
A non-refundable credit of $60 (single) or $120 (MFJ/HoH) for California renters whose California AGI does not exceed $52,044 (single) or $104,088 (MFJ). You must have rented for at least half the year.

## California Use Tax

If you purchased goods out-of-state (including online) without paying California sales tax, you owe California use tax on those purchases. You can report use tax directly on your Form 540, Line 91. The use tax rate equals the California sales tax rate applicable in your county (generally 7.25% or higher depending on local additions).

---

## Filing Requirements for California Nonresidents and Part-Year Residents

**Nonresidents** with California-source income exceeding certain thresholds must file Form 540NR.

**Filing thresholds for nonresidents (2024):**
- Single: California gross income over $20,824 OR California AGI over $16,659
- MFJ: California gross income over $41,648 OR California AGI over $33,318

**Part-year residents** always file Form 540NR and allocate income between the California-resident period and the nonresident period.

---

## Safe Harbor for California Nonresidents (Six-Month Rule)

California presumes you are a nonresident if you were outside California for an uninterrupted period of at least 546 consecutive days (18 months) under an employment-related contract. This safe harbor provides protection from California's aggressive residency rules during an extended assignment outside California.

---

## California Estimated Tax Payments

If you expect to owe more than $500 in California tax after withholding, you must make quarterly estimated tax payments to avoid underpayment penalties.

**2024 California Estimated Tax Due Dates:**
- 1st payment: April 15, 2024 (30% of annual estimate)
- 2nd payment: June 17, 2024 (40% of annual estimate)
- 3rd payment: No third quarter payment required
- 4th payment: January 15, 2025 (30% of annual estimate)

Note: California's schedule differs from the federal schedule — there is no third-quarter (September) payment. Percentages are 30%, 40%, 0%, 30% rather than equal 25% installments.

Use Form 540-ES to make estimated payments. You can also pay online at ftb.ca.gov.
