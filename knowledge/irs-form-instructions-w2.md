# IRS Form W-2 Instructions — Understanding Your Wage and Tax Statement

*Source: IRS Form W-2 Instructions and IRS Publication 15 (Tax Year 2024). For authoritative guidance, visit irs.gov.*

---

## What Is Form W-2?

Form W-2, Wage and Tax Statement, is issued by your employer and reports:
- The total wages, tips, and other compensation paid to you during the year
- The amount of federal, state, and local income tax withheld
- Social Security and Medicare taxes withheld
- Other compensation and benefits (such as retirement contributions)

Your employer must send your W-2 to you by **January 31** of the year following the tax year. You should receive a separate W-2 from each employer you worked for during the year.

---

## W-2 Box-by-Box Guide

### Box a: Employee's Social Security Number
Your SSN as shown on your Social Security card. If incorrect, contact your employer immediately to get a corrected W-2 (Form W-2c).

### Box b: Employer Identification Number (EIN)
Your employer's federal tax ID number, a 9-digit number in the format XX-XXXXXXX.

### Box c: Employer's Name, Address, and ZIP Code
The legal name and address of your employer.

### Box d: Control Number
An optional field your employer may use to identify the W-2 in their records. Not used on your tax return.

### Boxes e and f: Employee's Name and Address
Your name and address. If your name is incorrect, contact your employer for a corrected W-2.

---

## Key Income and Tax Boxes

### Box 1: Wages, Tips, Other Compensation
**The most important box.** This is the total taxable wages, tips, and other compensation paid to you by your employer. This amount goes on Line 1a of your Form 1040.

Box 1 does NOT include:
- Pre-tax contributions to a 401(k), 403(b), or similar retirement plan
- Pre-tax health insurance premiums (under a Section 125 cafeteria plan)
- Pre-tax Flexible Spending Account (FSA) contributions
- Pre-tax Health Savings Account (HSA) contributions through payroll
- Pre-tax commuter benefits (up to the monthly limit)

Box 1 DOES include:
- Taxable wages and salary
- Tips (including those reported to your employer)
- Bonuses and commissions
- Sick pay (if received from employer, not a third-party insurer)
- Taxable value of employer-provided life insurance over $50,000
- Taxable fringe benefits

### Box 2: Federal Income Tax Withheld
The total amount of federal income tax your employer withheld from your paychecks during the year. This amount is a payment toward your tax liability and is reported on Line 25a of Form 1040.

Your withholding is based on:
- Your Form W-4 elections (filing status, allowances, additional withholding)
- The IRS withholding tables

If too much was withheld, you'll receive a refund. If too little was withheld, you'll owe taxes and may face an underpayment penalty.

### Box 3: Social Security Wages
The amount of your wages subject to Social Security tax. This may differ from Box 1 because:
- Some items are included in Social Security wages but not federal wages (e.g., 401(k) contributions reduce Box 1 but NOT Box 3)
- Social Security wages are capped at the annual wage base ($168,600 for 2024)

### Box 4: Social Security Tax Withheld
The amount of Social Security tax withheld. For 2024, the employee rate is 6.2% of wages up to $168,600. Maximum amount: $10,453.20. If you had multiple employers and total Social Security wages exceeded $168,600, you may have had too much withheld — the excess is a refundable credit on your tax return.

### Box 5: Medicare Wages and Tips
The amount of your wages subject to Medicare tax. Unlike Social Security, there is NO cap on Medicare wages.

Medicare wages generally equal or exceed Box 3 because:
- There is no wage base limit for Medicare
- Certain employer-sponsored benefits excluded from Social Security wages are still subject to Medicare tax

### Box 6: Medicare Tax Withheld
The amount of Medicare tax withheld. The standard employee rate is 1.45% with no wage cap.

**Additional Medicare Tax:** If your wages (Box 5) plus all other wages exceed $200,000 (single) or $250,000 (MFJ), an additional 0.9% Medicare tax applies to the excess. Your employer withholds this additional tax only when your wages from that employer exceed $200,000 in a year. If you had multiple employers or a working spouse, you may owe additional Medicare tax not yet withheld — reported on Form 8959.

### Box 7: Social Security Tips
Cash tips you reported to your employer. These tips are included in Box 1 (federal wages) but are broken out separately here.

### Box 8: Allocated Tips
Tips allocated to you by your employer if your reported tips were less than the minimum required percentage of sales (usually 8%). These tips must be added to your wages on Form 1040.

### Box 10: Dependent Care Benefits
The total amount of dependent care benefits provided by your employer through a Dependent Care FSA or direct benefits. Up to $5,000 per year ($2,500 for MFS) is excludable from income. Amounts over the limit are taxable. Complete Form 2441 (Child and Dependent Care Expenses) to determine the taxable portion.

### Box 11: Nonqualified Deferred Compensation
Distributions from a nonqualified deferred compensation plan. This amount is already included in Box 1 wages. The purpose of this box is to satisfy ERISA reporting requirements.

### Box 12: Codes (up to four entries)

Box 12 uses single-letter codes to report various types of compensation and deductions:

| Code | Description |
|---|---|
| **D** | Contributions to a 401(k) plan (pre-tax) |
| **E** | Contributions to a 403(b) plan (pre-tax) |
| **G** | Contributions to a 457(b) governmental plan |
| **AA** | Designated Roth contributions to a 401(k) |
| **BB** | Designated Roth contributions to a 403(b) |
| **W** | Employer contributions (including employee) to an HSA |
| **DD** | Cost of employer-sponsored health coverage (informational only, not taxable) |
| **EE** | Designated Roth contributions under a governmental 457(b) plan |
| **FF** | Permitted benefits under a Qualified Small Employer Health Reimbursement Arrangement (QSEHRA) |
| **C** | Taxable cost of group-term life insurance over $50,000 |
| **J** | Non-taxable sick pay |
| **L** | Substantiated employee business expense reimbursements |
| **P** | Excludable moving expense reimbursements (military only) |
| **R** | Employer contributions to an Archer MSA |
| **S** | Employee salary reduction contributions to a SIMPLE IRA |
| **T** | Adoption benefits |
| **V** | Income from nonstatutory stock options |
| **Z** | Income under a nonqualified deferred compensation plan failing section 409A |

The amounts in Box 12 are informational and generally do NOT need to be entered separately on your 1040 — they are already included in (or excluded from) Box 1 as appropriate.

### Box 13: Checkboxes

**Statutory employee:** If checked, your wages are subject to Social Security and Medicare but not federal income tax withholding. Statutory employees report income and expenses on Schedule C.

**Retirement plan:** If checked, you were an active participant in your employer's retirement plan (401k, pension, etc.) during the year. This affects whether your traditional IRA contribution is deductible.

**Third-party sick pay:** If checked, sick pay was paid by a third party (e.g., insurance company) and may affect your withholding.

### Box 14: Other
Employers use this box to report various items that don't have a designated box. Common entries include:
- State disability insurance (SDI/SUI) taxes withheld
- Union dues
- Uniform payments
- Health insurance premiums for S-corp shareholders
- Educational assistance payments
- Domestic partner health benefits (imputed income)

## State and Local Tax Boxes (Boxes 15–20)

### Box 15: State and Employer's State ID Number
The two-letter state abbreviation and your employer's state tax ID.

### Box 16: State Wages, Tips, etc.
Your wages subject to state income tax. May differ from Box 1 because state tax laws differ from federal law. For example, some states do not exclude 401(k) contributions from state wages.

### Box 17: State Income Tax Withheld
Total state income tax withheld during the year. Enter on your state income tax return.

### Boxes 18–20: Local Wages and Tax Withheld
Used for local/city income taxes (e.g., New York City, Philadelphia). Report on your local tax return if applicable.

---

## Multiple W-2s

If you worked for multiple employers, you will receive a separate W-2 from each. Add the amounts from all W-2s when completing your 1040:
- Combine all Box 1 amounts for total wages
- Combine all Box 2 amounts for total federal tax withheld
- Combine all Box 3 amounts (for the excess Social Security tax credit calculation)

---

## What to Do If Your W-2 Is Wrong

**Contact your employer first.** Ask them to issue a corrected W-2 (Form W-2c).

**If your employer won't correct it or can't be reached:** Contact the IRS at 1-800-829-1040 after February 14th with your employer's name, address, and EIN, plus your wages and taxes withheld as best you know them.

**Filing with an incorrect W-2:** You can still file using the information you have. Attach Form 4852 (Substitute for Form W-2) and explain why you are using it.

## W-2 vs. Form 1099-NEC: Employee vs. Independent Contractor

If you receive a Form 1099-NEC (Nonemployee Compensation) instead of a W-2, your payer treats you as an independent contractor:
- **No withholding** — you are responsible for paying your own income tax and self-employment tax
- Report income on Schedule C
- Self-employment tax applies (15.3% on the first $168,600 of net earnings)
- You can deduct business expenses on Schedule C

If you believe you were misclassified as an independent contractor when you should be an employee, you can file Form SS-8 with the IRS for a determination.
