# Common US Tax Filing FAQs — Federal and California State

*Source: IRS.gov FAQs, IRS Publication 17, and California FTB guidance (Tax Year 2024). For authoritative guidance, visit irs.gov or ftb.ca.gov.*

---

## General Filing Questions

### Q: When is the federal tax return due?

**A:** Federal individual income tax returns (Form 1040) for tax year 2024 are due on **April 15, 2025**. If you live in Maine or Massachusetts, the deadline is April 17, 2025 (due to Patriots' Day and Emancipation Day holidays). Military members serving in combat zones receive additional time extensions.

### Q: How do I get a filing extension?

**A:** File Form 4868 by April 15, 2025 to get an automatic 6-month extension to **October 15, 2025**. You can file Form 4868 electronically through tax software, through the IRS Free File system, or by mailing a paper form. Important: an extension to file is NOT an extension to pay. If you owe taxes, you must still pay by April 15 to avoid late-payment penalties (0.5% per month) and interest. Estimate your tax liability and pay any balance due when you file Form 4868.

### Q: What happens if I miss the filing deadline?

**A:**
- **If you're owed a refund:** There is no penalty for filing late when you're owed a refund. However, you must file within 3 years of the original due date to claim your refund — after that, the refund is forfeited.
- **If you owe taxes:** The failure-to-file penalty is 5% of unpaid taxes per month (up to 25%). The failure-to-pay penalty is 0.5% per month (up to 25%). Interest accrues from the due date on any unpaid balance. These penalties can add up quickly — always file on time even if you can't pay the full amount.

### Q: Do I need to file a tax return if I have no income?

**A:** Generally, you are not required to file if your gross income is below the filing threshold for your filing status and age (see IRS Publication 17 knowledge document for 2024 thresholds). However, you may want to file even with no income to:
- Claim a refund of any taxes withheld
- Establish a tax filing record (useful for loans, visas, etc.)
- Claim the Earned Income Credit if you have earned income
- Claim the stimulus payment Recovery Rebate Credit if applicable

### Q: What is the difference between a deduction and a credit?

**A:**
- A **deduction** reduces your taxable income. If you're in the 22% tax bracket and claim a $1,000 deduction, you save $220 in taxes.
- A **credit** reduces your tax bill dollar for dollar. A $1,000 tax credit saves you exactly $1,000 regardless of your tax bracket. Credits are more valuable than deductions.
- **Refundable credits** (like the Earned Income Credit) can reduce your tax below zero and result in a refund even if you owe no tax. **Non-refundable credits** (like the Foreign Tax Credit) can only reduce your tax to zero.

## Income and Withholding

### Q: What is the difference between gross income, AGI, and taxable income?

**A:**
- **Gross income** is all income you received from all sources (wages, interest, dividends, self-employment, etc.) before any deductions.
- **Adjusted Gross Income (AGI)** is gross income minus certain "above-the-line" deductions (like IRA contributions, student loan interest, self-employed health insurance, alimony paid under pre-2019 agreements). AGI is calculated on Line 11 of Form 1040.
- **Taxable income** is AGI minus your standard deduction (or itemized deductions) and the Qualified Business Income (QBI) deduction. This is the income on which your federal tax is actually calculated.

### Q: My employer withheld too much tax. How do I get it back?

**A:** File your tax return and report all withholding on Line 25 of Form 1040. If your total payments (withholding + estimated payments + credits) exceed your total tax liability, the difference is your refund (Line 35a). The IRS issues most refunds within 21 days for e-filed returns. Choose direct deposit to get your refund faster.

### Q: I received a Form 1099-NEC. Do I have to report this income?

**A:** Yes. Form 1099-NEC reports nonemployee compensation (independent contractor income). You must report this income on Schedule C (Profit or Loss from Business). Net profit from Schedule C is subject to:
- Regular income tax (at your marginal rate)
- Self-employment tax (15.3% on the first $168,600 of net earnings; 2.9% on the excess)

You can deduct ordinary and necessary business expenses on Schedule C to reduce your net profit. Keep receipts and records of all business expenses.

### Q: I received a Form 1099-K. What do I do?

**A:** Form 1099-K reports payment card and third-party network transactions (Venmo, PayPal, Etsy, etc.). For tax year 2024, the IRS reporting threshold is $5,000 (reduced from $20,000/200 transactions). If the income is from a business or side gig, report it on Schedule C. If it represents the sale of personal items at a loss (e.g., selling used clothes), it is generally not taxable — you may need to show you sold the items for less than you paid.

### Q: I worked in multiple states. Do I have to file in all of them?

**A:** You may need to file a nonresident return in every state where you earned income. You'll also file a resident return in your home state. To prevent double taxation, most states allow a credit for taxes paid to other states. Generally, your home state taxes your worldwide income but gives you a credit for taxes you pay to other states on that same income.

## Self-Employment and Small Business

### Q: I have a side gig. When do I need to file a Schedule C?

**A:** You must file Schedule C and report all business income if you are self-employed or carry on a trade or business, even without a formal business entity. This applies to freelancers, consultants, gig workers (rideshare, delivery), artists, online sellers, tutors, and anyone who earns income outside of employment. There is no minimum threshold — you must report all self-employment income, including amounts under $400. However, self-employment tax only applies if your net self-employment income is $400 or more.

### Q: What is self-employment tax and how is it calculated?

**A:** Self-employment (SE) tax covers Social Security and Medicare taxes for self-employed individuals. Employees pay half these taxes (7.65%) and employers pay the other half; self-employed people pay both halves.

For 2024:
- **Social Security portion:** 12.4% on net self-employment income up to $168,600
- **Medicare portion:** 2.9% on all net self-employment income
- **Total SE tax rate:** 15.3% (on income up to the SS wage base) + 2.9% (on income above the SS wage base)
- **Additional Medicare Tax:** 0.9% on net self-employment income over $200,000 (single) or $250,000 (MFJ)

You calculate SE tax on Schedule SE. You can then deduct half of your SE tax (the "employer's share") as an above-the-line deduction on Schedule 1, Line 15.

### Q: What home office deductions can I claim?

**A:** If you use part of your home regularly and exclusively for business, you can deduct home office expenses. There are two methods:

**Simplified Method:** $5 per square foot of dedicated office space, up to 300 square feet (maximum deduction: $1,500). No depreciation tracking required.

**Regular Method:** Calculate the actual expenses for your home (mortgage interest or rent, utilities, insurance, repairs, depreciation) and multiply by the percentage of your home used for the office (office square footage ÷ total home square footage).

The space must be used **exclusively** for business — a spare bedroom with a desk that is also used as a guest room does not qualify.

## Retirement and Savings

### Q: What are the IRA contribution limits for 2024?

**A:** For tax year 2024:
- **Contribution limit:** $7,000 per person ($8,000 if age 50 or older)
- You can contribute to both a traditional and a Roth IRA, but the combined total cannot exceed the limit
- Contributions must be made by the tax filing deadline (April 15, 2025 for 2024 contributions)
- You must have earned income (wages, salaries, self-employment income) to contribute

### Q: What is the difference between a Traditional IRA and a Roth IRA?

**A:**
| Feature | Traditional IRA | Roth IRA |
|---|---|---|
| Contributions | May be tax-deductible | Not deductible |
| Growth | Tax-deferred | Tax-free |
| Withdrawals | Taxable at ordinary rates | Tax-free (if qualified) |
| RMDs | Required starting at age 73 | No RMDs during owner's lifetime |
| Income limits | Deductibility limited if covered by workplace plan | Contributions limited at high income |

**Roth IRA income limits for 2024:** Phase-out at $146,000–$161,000 MAGI (single) and $230,000–$240,000 MAGI (MFJ). Above these limits, you cannot contribute directly to a Roth IRA.

### Q: What are Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs)?

**A:** Starting at age 73, you must withdraw a minimum amount each year from traditional IRAs, 401(k)s, 403(b)s, and most other retirement accounts (but NOT Roth IRAs). The RMD is calculated by dividing your account balance at the end of the previous year by your life expectancy factor from the IRS Uniform Lifetime Table. Failure to take the RMD results in a 25% excise tax on the amount not withdrawn (reduced to 10% if you take the missed RMD within two years).

## Deductions and Credits

### Q: Should I take the standard deduction or itemize?

**A:** Take whichever is larger. The 2024 standard deduction is $14,600 (single) or $29,200 (MFJ). You should itemize if your total itemized deductions exceed your standard deduction. Common itemized deductions include:
- State and local taxes (SALT): capped at $10,000 federal
- Mortgage interest on loans up to $750,000
- Charitable contributions (cash and non-cash)
- Medical expenses exceeding 7.5% of AGI
- Casualty losses in federally declared disaster areas

Most taxpayers take the standard deduction after the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017 significantly raised it.

### Q: How does the Child Tax Credit work?

**A:** For 2024, the Child Tax Credit is up to $2,000 per qualifying child under age 17 at year-end. A qualifying child must:
- Be your child, stepchild, adopted child, sibling, or descendant
- Be under age 17 on December 31, 2024
- Have lived with you for more than half the year
- Have a valid Social Security number
- Not have provided more than half of their own support

Up to $1,700 of the $2,000 credit is refundable (the "Additional Child Tax Credit" or ACTC). The credit phases out beginning at $200,000 MAGI (single) and $400,000 MAGI (MFJ).

### Q: What charitable donations can I deduct?

**A:** You can deduct contributions to qualified charitable organizations (501(c)(3) nonprofits) if you itemize deductions. Requirements:
- Cash donations: Keep a bank record or written receipt. Donations of $250+ require a written acknowledgment from the charity.
- Non-cash donations (clothing, household goods): Deduct fair market value. For items worth more than $500, file Form 8283. For items worth more than $5,000 (other than publicly traded securities), get a qualified appraisal.
- Mileage for charitable driving: 14 cents per mile for 2024.

**Limits:** Cash donations to public charities are generally deductible up to 60% of AGI. Appreciated property donations are limited to 30% of AGI. Excess contributions carry forward up to 5 years.

## Crypto and Digital Assets

### Q: Do I owe taxes on cryptocurrency?

**A:** Yes. The IRS treats cryptocurrency and other digital assets as property. Tax consequences include:
- **Selling crypto:** Capital gain or loss (short-term if held ≤1 year, long-term if held >1 year)
- **Trading one crypto for another:** Taxable exchange — triggers capital gain/loss
- **Using crypto to pay for goods/services:** Taxable event based on fair market value at time of transaction
- **Receiving crypto as payment for services:** Ordinary income at fair market value when received
- **Mining or staking rewards:** Generally ordinary income at fair market value when received

You must answer "Yes" to the digital assets question on Line 1 of Form 1040 if any of the above apply to you.

## IRS Notices and Audits

### Q: I received a letter from the IRS. What should I do?

**A:** Don't panic. The IRS sends letters for many reasons — most are not audits. Common notices include:
- **CP2000:** IRS noticed a discrepancy between your return and information reported by third parties. Respond by the deadline with documentation.
- **CP14:** Balance due notice. Pay or set up a payment plan.
- **LT11 / CP90:** Final notice before levy action. Take immediate action.
- **Letter 531:** Deficiency notice (you have 90 days to file a petition in Tax Court or agree to the proposed change).

Always respond to IRS correspondence promptly. You can also check your IRS account online at IRS.gov to see your transcripts, notices, and payment history.

### Q: What triggers a tax audit?

**A:** The IRS selects returns for audit based on a variety of factors, including:
- Mathematical errors
- Missing income (1099s or W-2s not reported)
- Unusually high deductions relative to income
- High Schedule C losses (especially for multiple years)
- Large charitable deductions relative to income
- Home office deductions for W-2 employees
- 100% business use of a vehicle
- Large cash transactions

The overall individual audit rate is less than 1%, but it increases significantly for higher-income returns and self-employed individuals with Schedule C losses.
